Israelis started reviving a flashpoint outpost settlement in the occupied West Bank on Monday, AFP journalists said, constructing a building at the site which has drawn international attention.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Using a truck, diggers and an earth roller, work got underway to erect a portable building at the northern West Bank site.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

AFP journalists saw Israeli soldiers guarding around 20 people carrying out construction work at the Homesh site, which Israel evacuated nearly two decades ago.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The latest development comes weeks after the office of Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu pledged his government had no plans to reconstruct the site, after a parliamentary vote on the matter sparked ire abroad.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

It was hailed as a “moving historic moment” by Israel’s National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir — an extreme-right settler.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Lawmakers in March voted to annul part of a law which banned Israelis from living in areas of the West Bank which the state evacuated in 2005, the same year Israel withdrew from the Gaza Strip.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“The government has no intention of establishing new communities in these areas,” Netanyahu’s office said in March.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Israel’s military occupation of the West Bank has been in force since the 1967 Six-Day War.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Neither the army nor the defence ministry commented on their role in Monday’s developments at Homesh when contacted by AFP.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The civilians at the site refused to discuss the matter when approached by AFP journalists.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The United States, Israel’s top ally, said this month it was “deeply troubled by the Israeli government’s order that allows its citizens to establish a permanent presence in the Homesh outpost”.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Washington and the wider international community view Israeli settlements built in the Palestinian territories as illegal and a barrier to peace.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
